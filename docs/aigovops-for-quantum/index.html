Framework

An AiGovOps-style control stack for quantum.

1. Visibility

Inventory cryptography, quantum-adjacent vendors, pilots, claims, and workloads. You cannot govern what you cannot enumerate.

2. Classification

Classify systems by cryptographic dependence, data longevity, criticality, and quantum sensitivity. Not every system deserves the same urgency.

3. Policy as code

Encode migration rules, control requirements, and procurement gates so governance becomes executable and testable, not a static PDF.

4. Evidence

Generate attestable artifacts: inventories, exception lists, vendor declarations, migration scorecards, and readiness reports.

5. Continuous assurance

Re-run checks as architecture changes, certificates roll, vendors update roadmaps, or federal standards evolve.

6. Response

Maintain runbooks for PQC migration, supplier changes, and “advantage claim” reviews so leaders can act before hype hardens into risk.

Key extension of AiGovOps thinking: AI governance often starts with models and data. Quantum governance starts with cryptography, system lifetimes, vendor posture, and proof thresholds for technical claims.

Interactive primer

Short-form teaching content for non-quantum technical readers.

Step 1 — What quantum is: a specialized kind of computing built from qubits rather than ordinary bits. It is not faster at everything; it is potentially better at some problem classes.
Step 2 — Why it matters: chemistry, some optimization, and public-key cryptography are the headline domains. Crypto risk matters first because data stolen now may be decrypted later.
Step 3 — Why it is hard: qubits are fragile, and error correction is expensive. The real race is not “who has the most qubits,” but who can produce useful logical qubits at viable overhead.
Step 4 — The five approaches: superconducting, trapped-ion, neutral-atom, photonic, and topological. Each is a different engineering answer to the same problem: build reliable logical qubits.
Step 5 — Good, bad, maybe impossible: good means new capability in science and optimization; bad means broken public-key cryptography and strategic surprise; maybe impossible means skeptics are right that scalable fault tolerance never escapes the lab.
Step 6 — What to do now: do not buy quantum hardware. Inventory cryptography, assign owners, classify sensitive systems, and define what evidence would count as a real breakthrough for your organization.
White paper core

A concise publishable thesis for “AiGovOps for Quantum — Alpha Thinking.”

Abstract: Quantum computing should be governed now, not because fault-tolerant utility is broadly available today, but because cryptographic migration, supplier posture, system longevity, and evidence standards for breakthrough claims already create governance obligations.

Core arguments

  • Quantum governance begins with cryptographic visibility, not speculative compute buying.
  • Federal policy now supplies operational deadlines that can anchor enterprise programs.
  • Quantum claims need reproducibility thresholds and proof standards before they influence material spend.
  • AiGovOps patterns transfer well: policies as code, evidence pipelines, ownership, exceptions, and continuous assurance.
  • The most mature early use case is PQC migration governance, not broad quantum application deployment.
